- **Labelled paragraph structure**, the standard in computer science and empirical software engineering theses. The canonical structure is:
- ### Template
    - **Objective paragraph** — what question or gap the chapter addressed and why.
    - **Method paragraph** — how it was addressed, at a level of abstraction above the procedural detail.
    - **Findings paragraph** — what the results showed, including key interpretive claims.
    - **Implications paragraph** — what the results mean for research, tooling, and practice.
- ### Objective Paragraph
    - **Template:** ^^[Domain or artifact type]^^ typically ^^[describe the gap as a condition — what is missing, conflated, or omitted]^^. Because ^^[consequence of that condition — what cannot be done or distinguished as a result]^^, this chapter ^^[verb: constructs / develops / proposes / analyzes]^^ ^^[the object — what was built or done, stated conceptually]^^ that ^^[what property or capability it provides]^^. Concretely, the chapter addresses __^^[RQ verbatim]^^__.
        - **Gap as condition.** State what the domain, artifact, or prior work typically does or omits. No argument for why it matters yet — just the observed state of affairs.
        - **Chapter response.** Open with "Because [consequence]" to connect the gap to the response, then state what the chapter does in one conceptual clause. No method, no pipeline, no dataset sizes.
        - **RQ anchor.** Verbatim, introduced with "Concretely" to signal the shift from prose framing to formal question.
- ### Findings Paragraph
    - **Template:** ^^[Scope/yield]^^: ^^[fraction or subset obtained, with key numbers]^^, with ^^[exclusions]^^ due to ^^[reason]^^ rather than ^^[alternative explanation that would threaten validity]^^. ^^[Finding 1 — result with key numbers]^^. This ^^[finding 1 noun]^^ shows that ^^[conceptual claim 1]^^, a ^^[pattern/structure]^^ that ^^[finding 2 noun]^^ ^^[complements/supports/extends]^^: ^^[Finding 2 — result with key numbers]^^. This ^^[finding 2 noun]^^ shows that ^^[conceptual claim 2]^^, a ^^[relationship]^^ that ^^[finding 3 noun]^^ further ^^[articulates/reveals/confirms]^^: ^^[Finding 3 — result with key numbers]^^. This ^^[overall pattern]^^ shows that ^^[culminating claim]^^, and that ^^[what this requires of the object under study]^^, thereby answering ^^[RQ verbatim or by number]^^.
        - A **scope or yield statement** that anchors the empirical basis — what was obtained, at what scale, and (briefly) why anything is missing, without yet making a substantive claim. This grounds the reader before the argument starts.
        - A **chain of empirical findings**, each paired with a light conceptual gloss — not full interpretation (that belongs in the discussion or implications), but enough to name what the result establishes so the next finding can build on it. The progression should mirror the order of the results subsections.
        - A **culminating synthesis** that ties the chain together and explicitly answers the RQ. This is where the paragraph's interpretive work peaks.
    - **Rule of thumb:** each "this [X] shows that [Y]" clause should state what the result __establishes__ (a fact about the object of study), not what should be __done__ about it (that is the implications paragraph's job). The chain length should match the number of findings boxes in your results section — three or four is typical; more than that tends to overload a single paragraph and may warrant splitting findings into two paragraphs.
- ### Implications Paragraph
    - An implications paragraph stays grounded in what the chapter's results mean for people working with the artifact, without prescribing what happens next in the thesis.
    - **Template A — explicit causal form:** Because ^^[finding 1]^^, ^^[audience 1]^^ must/should ^^[consequence]^^, since ^^[risk or reasoning if the consequence is ignored]^^. Because ^^[finding 2]^^, ^^[audience 2]^^ must/should ^^[consequence]^^, with ^^[qualifier — e.g., which part remains least addressed]^^. Because ^^[finding 3]^^, ^^[audience 3]^^ ^^[consequence — often a caveat about how to treat the artifact/results]^^, and ^^[boundary condition]^^.
    - **Template B — audience-labeled form:** These results suggest that ^^[consequence for evaluation/research practice]^^, since ^^[reasoning]^^, and ^^[secondary risk]^^ risk ^^[consequence of ignoring it]^^. For ^^[audience 2]^^, ^^[finding-grounded claim about viability/capability]^^, with ^^[qualifier about a limitation or risk introduced]^^. For ^^[audience 3]^^, ^^[reframing of how the artifact/tool should be used in practice]^^, and ^^[additional practical implication or entry point]^^.
    - **One sentence per audience/stakeholder group**, mirroring the implications subsections in the discussion (here: empirical research, tooling/automation, practice/maintainers). The order typically follows the same order as those subsections.
    - **Each sentence traces a finding to a consequence** for that audience — what changes, what must be done differently, or what risk emerges, given what the chapter found. No new findings are introduced here; everything must be traceable to the Findings paragraph.
    - **Each sentence carries a qualifier or boundary condition** — "since," "risk," "with the caveat that," "remains the least addressed" — which keeps the implication honest rather than overclaiming. This is where the hedging from the findings paragraph propagates forward.
    - **No forward references to subsequent chapters** — implications stay grounded in what __this__ chapter's results mean for that audience, not in how the thesis proceeds.
- Limitations and future work do not typically appear in the summary — they belong in the discussion.
- Some theses add a fifth labelled paragraph:
- > **Contributions** — what the chapter adds to the thesis as a whole, connecting forward to subsequent chapters or backward to earlier ones.
